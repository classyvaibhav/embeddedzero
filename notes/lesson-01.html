Electricity Basics & Ohm's Law

Voltage, current, resistance, power. How to light up an LED without blowing it. The single most-used formula in all of electronics.

By the end of this lesson, you will:
  • Explain V, I, R, P in plain words and tell conventional current from electron flow
  • Use Ohm's law to calculate the right resistor for an LED
  • Build an LED circuit in Tinkercad and watch it light up

1The Four Things You Measure

Everything in electronics comes down to four quantities. Let's meet them.

⚙ Your Physics Brain Already Knows This Remember V = W/q from your EM course? That's voltage — energy per unit charge. We'll build on what you already know. Just swap the notation to engineering units.

Voltage (V)

The "pressure" that pushes charges around. Measured in volts. Think of it like water pressure — the taller the tank, the harder the water is pushed through the pipe.

Voltage is always a difference between two points. When someone says "5 volts", they mean "5 volts above ground".

Current (I)

The rate at which charges flow. Measured in amperes (A). In our water analogy, this is the flow rate — litres per second through the pipe.

Important: conventional current flows from + to − (from high voltage to low), but electrons actually flow the other way. Just a convention Ben Franklin got wrong 200 years ago — we're stuck with it.

Resistance (R)

The opposition to current flow. Measured in ohms (Ω). In the water analogy, a narrow pipe has high resistance; a wide pipe has low resistance.

Power (P)

The rate of energy transfer. Measured in watts (W). A 1-watt device uses 1 joule of energy every second.

Water analogy on the left, circuit equivalent on the right. They behave the same way.
📄 Key Relationships
  • Voltage → what drives the current
  • Current → what flows through the circuit
  • Resistance → what opposes the flow
  • Power → the rate at which energy is used (as heat, light, motion)

2Ohm's Law — The Most Famous Equation

For ohmic materials (like resistors), voltage and current are directly proportional. The constant of proportionality is the resistance:

V = I · R

Rearrange it three ways:

V = I·R   ·   I = V / R   ·   R = V / I

And the three forms of power:

P = V·I  =  I²·R  =  V²/R
⚠ Heads Up Ohm's law only works for linear components — resistors, plain wires. It does not apply to diodes, LEDs, or transistors. For those, you use Ohm's law on the resistor in series with them, not on them directly.

3The LED Problem (Classic Example)

An LED needs a certain voltage across it to light up (~2V for a red LED), and a certain current flowing through it (~20 mA is typical). If you connect it directly to 5V, it will either burn out or cause excessive current. You need a current-limiting resistor in series.

Here's how to calculate it:

  1. Figure out the voltage across the resistor: VR = Vsupply − VLED = 5V − 2V = 3V
  2. Apply Ohm's law: R = VR / I = 3V / 0.020A = 150 Ω
  3. Check the power dissipation: P = VR · I = 3 × 0.020 = 60 mW
  4. Pick a resistor rating: A standard 1/4 watt (250 mW) resistor handles 60 mW easily.
💡 Engineer's Shortcut For most LEDs from 5V supply, use 220 Ω (gives ~13 mA, slightly dim but safe). For 3.3V supply, use 150 Ω. Memorise these two values — they cover 90% of hobby LED work.
💻 Try It Live

Light up an LED in Tinkercad

Let's build the circuit without any real hardware. Tinkercad Circuits runs in your browser — free, no install.

Step-by-step:
  1. Open tinkercad.com/circuits (free account; signs in with Google)
  2. Click "Create new Circuit"
  3. Drag a 9V battery, a resistor (set to 220 Ω), and an LED (red) onto the canvas
  4. Click the resistor, change "Resistance" to 220 Ω in the panel
  5. Connect: battery + → resistor → LED anode (longer leg); LED cathode (shorter leg) → battery −
  6. Click "Start Simulation" in the top-right
  7. 🎉 The LED lights up! Turn on the multimeter and measure the current — it should be ~13 mA.

4Work This Out Yourself

Solve these three problems with paper and pen before you check the answers. This is where understanding becomes instinct.

ProblemSupply VLED VfDesired IWhat R?
1. Green LED on Arduino 5V5 V2.2 V15 mA?
2. Blue LED on ESP32 3.3V3.3 V3.0 V10 mA?
3. Red LED on 12V car battery12 V2.0 V20 mA?

🔍 Check Yourself

Problem 1 — Green LED on 5V, Vf=2.2V, I=15 mA
R = (Vs − Vf) / I = (5 − 2.2) / 0.015 = 186 Ω.
Nearest E12 standard value: 180 Ω (slightly above, fewer current). Or 220 Ω (safer, slightly dimmer).
Problem 2 — Blue LED on 3.3V, Vf=3.0V, I=10 mA
R = (3.3 − 3.0) / 0.010 = 30 Ω.
Nearest E12: 33 Ω. Close to the margin — 3.3V supplies with 3V LEDs don't have much headroom. Adjust if LED doesn't light.
Problem 3 — Red LED on 12V, Vf=2V, I=20 mA
R = (12 − 2) / 0.020 = 500 Ω.
Nearest E12: 470 Ω. Power dissipated: P = 10 × 0.020 = 0.2 W. A 1/4 W resistor is borderline — use 1/2 W to be safe.
Why doesn't Ohm's law work on the LED directly?
The LED's current-voltage curve is exponential, not linear. Ohm's law (V=IR) assumes linearity. That's why you always need a separate resistor in series to limit current; the resistor is where Ohm's law does its work.
Electrons flow from where to where in a battery-resistor loop?
Electrons flow from the − terminal, through the wire and resistor, to the + terminal — the opposite direction of the conventional current arrow. Both conventions are valid; we use "conventional" because it was named first (even though it's physically backwards).

Key Takeaways

  • V (volts) = voltage between two points; I (amps) = current through a wire; R (ohms) = resistance; P (watts) = power.
  • Ohm's law: V = I · R. Can rearrange to find any missing value.
  • Power: P = V · I = I²R = V²/R. Always check your resistor's wattage rating.
  • To light an LED: pick R = (Vsupply − Vf) / I.
  • Ohm's law only works for linear components. LEDs, diodes, transistors don't follow it directly.
  • Conventional current flows + → −; electrons actually flow the opposite way. Just a naming convention.
