1The Humble Resistor

The most common component on any board. Its only job: limit current. Simple but essential.

Reading the Colour Code

Every resistor has coloured bands printed on it. Four bands is standard. Read them left-to-right, with the tolerance band (gold or silver) always on the right:

ColourDigitMultiplierTolerance
Black0×1—
Brown1×10±1%
Red2×100±2%
Orange3×1,000—
Yellow4×10,000—
Green5×100,000±0.5%
Blue6×1,000,000±0.25%
Violet7×10M±0.1%
Gold—×0.1±5%
Silver—×0.01±10%
A 10 kΩ resistor: Brown (1), Black (0), Orange (×1000), Gold (±5%).
💡 Memorise This Rhyme "B. B. Roy of Great Britain has a Very Good Wife" — Black, Brown, Red, Orange, Yellow, Green, Blue, Violet, Grey, White. That's the digits 0–9 in order.

Standard Values — You Won't See "11.3 kΩ" in Real Life

Resistors come in standard E12 values: 10, 12, 15, 18, 22, 27, 33, 39, 47, 56, 68, 82 — and their decade multiples (100, 120, 150, etc.). When you calculate "need 186 Ω", you pick the nearest E12 value: 180 or 220.

Series and Parallel

Series: Rtotal = R1 + R2 + ...
Parallel: 1/Rtotal = 1/R1 + 1/R2 + ...  ·  Or for two: R1R2/(R1+R2)

Example: Two 10 kΩ resistors in parallel = 5 kΩ. Two 10 kΩ in series = 20 kΩ.

2Capacitors — Little Charge Buckets

A capacitor stores charge. When you apply voltage across it, charge flows in. When you remove the voltage, charge flows out. Capacitance is measured in farads (F), but real-world values are tiny: picofarads (pF), nanofarads (nF), and microfarads (μF).

⚙ Physics Link Q = C · V. Sound familiar? Same equation you saw in your EM physics course. A 100 nF cap at 5V stores Q = 10-7 × 5 = 500 nC of charge. Tiny but enough to matter.

Three Types You'll Actually Use

TypeValuesPolarity?What it's for
Ceramic (MLCC)1 pF – 10 μFNoDecoupling, HF filtering (most common)
Electrolytic1 μF – 10 mFYes!Bulk power filtering
Tantalum0.1 μF – 1 mFYes!Stable power rails
⚠ Never Reverse an Electrolytic Cap Electrolytic capacitors have a stripe on the side marking the negative terminal. If you install it backwards, it can literally explode. This is not a joke — I've seen it happen. Always double-check.

The 100 nF "Decoupling" Trick

Every single IC on every single board needs a 100 nF ceramic capacitor placed as close as possible to its Vcc pin. Why? At high switching frequencies, this cap acts as a "local charge reservoir" that smooths out noise before it can cause glitches.

This is the single most-important rule in all of electronics design. Learn to spot these caps on every schematic — if you don't see them, the board is broken.

3The Voltage Divider — Most-Used Formula Ever

Take two resistors in series across a voltage. The voltage at the midpoint is a fraction of the input, scaled by the resistor values:

Vout = Vin × R2 / (R1 + R2)
Classic voltage divider. Vout = 5 × 3.3/(1.8+3.3) = 3.24V — perfect for an ESP32 ADC input.
👁 Where You'll Use This
  • Scaling a 0–5V sensor down to 0–3.3V for an ESP32 ADC input
  • Reading a battery voltage through an ADC (if Vbat > Vref)
  • Making a reference voltage for a comparator
  • Biasing a sensor (like an LDR or thermistor)
⚠ Watch Out: Loading Effect Voltage dividers are great for signal inputs (where almost no current flows), but useless for power. If you try to power a whole circuit through a divider, the current drawn will crash the output voltage. For power, use a regulator instead.

Quick Example

Problem: You have a sensor that outputs 0–5V. Your ESP32 ADC can only handle 0–3.3V. What divider do you use?

  1. You need Vout/Vin = 3.3/5 = 0.66
  2. So R2 / (R1 + R2) = 0.66
  3. Solving: R1/R2 = (1/0.66) − 1 = 0.515
  4. Pick R2 = 10 kΩ, then R1 = 5.15 kΩ — use standard 4.7 kΩ. Or: R1 = 1.8 kΩ, R2 = 3.3 kΩ (from the diagram above).

🔍 Check Yourself

Red-Red-Brown-Gold — what's the value?
Red (2), Red (2), Brown (×10), Gold (±5%) = 220 Ω ±5%. A very common resistor for current-limiting LEDs.
A cap marked "104" — what does that mean?
Ceramic cap marking: first two digits are the value, third is the multiplier in picofarads. So "104" = 10 × 104 pF = 100,000 pF = 100 nF (0.1 μF). The most-common decoupling cap value ever.
You need 75 Ω, but E12 values only have 68 and 82. Which do you pick for an LED?
Pick 82 Ω (higher). Higher R means slightly less current, which is safer — the LED will be slightly dimmer but won't be over-driven. Going with 68 Ω would give slightly too much current.
Voltage divider: Vin=9V, R1=10k, R2=20k. Vout=?
Vout = 9 × 20/(10+20) = 9 × 2/3 = 6V.
Why can't you use a voltage divider to convert 12V down to 5V to power an MCU?
Because an MCU draws real current. When the MCU starts pulling 50–100 mA, the resistors in the divider will drop huge voltages (V=IR) — enough to crash the midpoint voltage way below 5V. Dividers only work for signal inputs (where very little current flows). For power, use a regulator chip like AMS1117-5.

Key Takeaways

  • Resistors use colour codes — read left to right, tolerance band on the right.
  • Standard E12 values: 10, 12, 15, 18, 22, 27, 33, 39, 47, 56, 68, 82 (and decade multiples).
  • Series: add resistances. Parallel: 1/Rtotal = sum of 1/R (or use R1R2/(R1+R2) for two).
  • Ceramic caps for decoupling/filtering, electrolytics for bulk power. Never reverse electrolytics!
  • Every IC needs a 100 nF ceramic cap right next to its Vcc pin. Always. No exceptions.
  • Voltage divider: Vout = Vin · R2/(R1+R2). Used everywhere for level-shifting signals.
  • Dividers are for signals, not for power. Use regulators for power.
