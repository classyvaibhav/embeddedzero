1Diodes — The Electrical Check Valve

A diode is the simplest semiconductor device: current flows one way, but not the other. Think of it like a one-way valve in plumbing.

⚙ You Already Know the Physics From your semiconductor physics course: a diode is a PN junction. Forward bias lowers the barrier and electrons flow easily. Reverse bias widens the depletion region and blocks current. That's it — we're just putting that theory to practical use.

Key Numbers to Memorise

Every diode has a forward voltage drop Vf. This is the voltage lost across the diode when it conducts:

Schottky (low-drop)0.3 VPower, reverse-polarity protection1N5819

The diode's current-voltage curve. Nothing flows until Vf, then it conducts exponentially.

2Why LEDs Always Need a Resistor

You met this in Lesson 1 — here's the why. An LED's I-V curve is exponential. A tiny change in voltage above Vf causes a huge jump in current. There's no "self-limiting" behaviour. Without a resistor, the LED pulls enormous current, heats up, and dies in seconds.

The resistor does the current limiting: it has a linear relationship, so the voltage drop across it scales with current. This creates a stable operating point.

LED Without a Resistor (in simulation only!)

In real life, connecting an LED directly to 5V blows it in seconds. In a simulator you can actually do this and watch it happen — safely.

Try this experiment:

3Transistors — Switches That MCUs Can Control

Your ESP32's GPIO pins output 3.3V, but only at ~20 mA. You can light an LED directly, but you cannot run a motor, relay, or bright LED strip — they need much more current. So we use a transistor as a switch: the tiny GPIO signal controls a big current path to V+.

💡 Easy Rule Small stuff (<100 mA): BJT like 2N2222. Big stuff: logic-level N-MOSFET like IRLZ44N. "Logic-level" is important: it means the MOSFET fully turns on at 3.3V or 5V (not just 10V).

Wiring a BJT Switch

NPN BJT as a low-side switch. When GPIO goes HIGH, transistor turns on, load gets power.

Sizing the Base Resistor

Rule of thumb: you want the transistor fully "saturated" (on as hard as possible). Drive ~10× more base current than strictly needed:

RB = (VGPIO − 0.7V) / IB
where IB = (Iload / β) × 10

Example: Drive a 100 mA relay with a 2N2222 (β=100) from 3.3V GPIO. IB = (0.1/100) × 10 = 10 mA. RB = (3.3−0.7)/0.010 = 260 Ω → pick 220 Ω.

⚠ The Flyback Diode Rule Whenever you switch an inductive load (relay coil, motor, solenoid), you must add a reverse-biased diode across the load. When the transistor turns off, the inductor generates a huge voltage spike that will destroy the transistor. The diode absorbs that spike safely. Use a 1N4007 or similar, cathode (stripe) pointing toward V+.

Control a Motor with a MOSFET in Wokwi

Build this in Wokwi:

  2. Add: IRLZ44N MOSFET, DC motor, 1N4007 diode, 12V battery (external power)

🔍 Check Yourself

Why can't you connect a 5V signal backwards through an LED?
Backwards = reverse-biased. Diodes block reverse current (until breakdown voltage, which destroys normal diodes). So no current flows, and no light.
You need to switch a 2A motor from an ESP32 GPIO. BJT or MOSFET?
Logic-level N-MOSFET (like IRLZ44N). A 2N2222 BJT is rated 800 mA — you'd burn it at 2A. MOSFETs handle tens of amps easily.
What does "logic-level" mean for a MOSFET?
The MOSFET fully turns on with a low gate voltage (3.3V or 5V). A standard MOSFET needs 10V on the gate, which an MCU can't deliver. Always buy logic-level MOSFETs for MCU-driven designs.
Why do you need a flyback diode across a relay coil?
The relay coil is inductive. When the MOSFET/BJT switches off, the coil's stored magnetic field collapses and creates a huge reverse voltage spike (hundreds of volts). The flyback diode shorts that spike safely, protecting the transistor from destruction.

Key Takeaways

  • A diode conducts only one way. Forward Vf: Si ~0.7V, Schottky ~0.3V, LEDs ~2–3V.
  • LEDs always need a current-limiting resistor — they're not ohmic.
  • Use a BJT (like 2N2222) to switch small loads <100 mA.
  • Use a logic-level N-MOSFET (like IRLZ44N) to switch big loads from a 3.3V MCU.
  • BJTs need a base resistor. MOSFETs don't (but benefit from a small gate resistor).
  • Always add a flyback diode across any inductive load (relays, motors).
