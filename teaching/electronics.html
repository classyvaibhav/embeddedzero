T1.1

Voltage, Current, Resistance & Power

Session 1 · 45 min
Goal: She can explain V, I, R, P in her own words and tell you the direction of conventional current vs electron flow.

The four variables that govern everything. Anchor her physics brain: voltage is potential energy per unit charge (like water pressure), current is flow rate of charge (like water flow), resistance opposes that flow (pipe narrowness), power is rate of energy transfer.

She already knows V = W/q from physics. The job is to remap those symbols onto engineering units and signs.

Simple loop — conventional current flows out of the + terminal.
V = voltage [V]   I = current [A]   R = resistance [Ω]   P = power [W]
Quick example: 9V battery drives 9 mA through 1 kΩ. P = V×I = 81 mW. (1/4 W resistor handles easily.)
Teach this way
  1. Water analogy on whiteboard: tank height = voltage, pipe flow = current, narrowness = resistance, work done = power.
  2. Redraw same circuit electrically side-by-side.
  3. Ask: "If I double the battery voltage, what happens to current?" — segues to Ohm's law.
  4. Point out conventional current flows + → − but electrons go opposite. Everyone gets this confused once — address head-on.
Common confusion
  • Mixing up voltage across a component vs potential at a node. Hammer: voltage is always a difference.
  • Unit prefixes (mA, μA, mV). Drill: 0.005 A = 5 mA, 2 mV = 0.002 V.
  • Current flows through, voltage across, power dissipates. Correct her language every time.
Verify understanding
  1. Current through a 330 Ω resistor on 5 V? 15.15 mA
  2. 1 W resistor dissipates how many J/s? 1 J/s = 1 W
  3. Electrons flow from where to where? − terminal → + terminal (opposite of conv. current)
T1.2

Ohm's Law

Session 2 · 45 min
Goal: She solves V=IR in any direction, picks a current-limit resistor for any LED, explains why Ohm's law doesn't apply to diodes.

For linear (ohmic) components, V ∝ I with constant of proportionality R. Combine with P = VI for three power forms: P = VI = I²R = V²/R.

V = I · R   I = V / R   R = V / I
P = V·I = I²·R = V²/R
Classic LED example: Red LED Vf=2.0 V, If=20 mA, supply 5 V. R = (5−2)/0.02 = 150 Ω. PR = 60 mW (1/4 W fine).
Teach this way
  1. Walk the LED example on whiteboard. Give her 3 different LEDs + supplies; she solves.
  2. Build on breadboard: 5V → 150 Ω → LED. Let her wire. Measure I with DMM in series.
  3. Swap 1kΩ → LED dim. Swap 68Ω → calculate (would exceed rating).
  4. Mention: LEDs aren't ohmic. Vf stays constant. Resistor drops the rest.
Common confusion
  • Applying V=IR across the LED itself. Won't work.
  • Missing power check — she'll calculate R but skip wattage. Always check P.
  • Confusing voltage drop direction. Current enters higher-potential side of a resistor.
Verify understanding
  1. Pick R for green LED (Vf=2.2 V, If=15 mA) from 3.3 V. (3.3-2.2)/0.015 = 73 Ω → use 82 Ω
  2. Power dissipated by 220 Ω with 50 mA? 0.55 W → need 1 W resistor
  3. Why does Ohm's law fail for a diode? I–V is exponential, not linear
Reference: Adafruit — LED Forward Voltage and KVL
T1.3

Kirchhoff's Laws (KCL & KVL)

Session 3 · 40 min
Goal: She writes loop equations (KVL) and node equations (KCL) for a 3-resistor mixed network.

KCL (nodes): Sum of currents into any node = 0. Charge conservation.

KVL (loops): Sum of voltage drops around any closed loop = 0. Energy conservation.

These two + Ohm's law solve any passive linear circuit. The intuition matters more than formal mesh analysis.

KCL at a node: currents in = currents out.
KCL: Σ Iin = Σ Iout (at every node)
KVL: Σ Vrises = Σ Vdrops (around every loop)
Teach this way
  1. Whiteboard single-loop: 9V + 1k, 2k, 3k series. KVL: 9 = I(1k+2k+3k) → I = 1.5 mA. Verify drops sum to 9V.
  2. Add parallel branch at a node. Apply KCL.
  3. Stick to sign convention: "clockwise, add rises, subtract drops."
  4. She solves a 2-loop circuit on her own. Sign errors are universal; correct gently.
Common confusion
  • Sign errors. 90% of KVL mistakes. Emphasise: going through a resistor in the direction of assumed current flow is a voltage drop.
  • Forgetting that KVL works around any loop, not just outer.
Verify understanding
  1. 12V source, resistors drop 3V, 5V, 4V — consistent? Yes: 3+5+4=12 ✓
  2. Currents into node: 10, 6, −4 mA. Net outflow? 12 mA
  3. Can KVL be violated in DC? No, ever. Re-check signs.
T1.4

Voltage & Current Dividers

Session 4 · 45 min
Goal: Derive Vout = Vin·R2/(R1+R2) from scratch; explain why loading changes output.

Most-used formula in practical electronics. Sensor biasing (LDR, thermistor), level-shifting 5V→3.3V, reference voltages, op-amp feedback.

Classic 2-resistor voltage divider.
Vout = Vin · R2 / (R1 + R2)
Current divider: I1 = Itotal · R2 / (R1 + R2)
Level-shift: Sensor 0–5V → ESP32 0–3.3V. R1=1.8k, R2=3.3k. At 5V: Vout = 5×3.3/5.1 = 3.24V. Safe.
Teach this way
  1. Derive: I = Vin/(R1+R2), then Vout = I·R2. She retains better if she derived it.
  2. Breadboard: two 10k + 5V. Measure ~2.5V. Works.
  3. Attach 1k load across R2. Vout drops significantly. Teaches output impedance / loading.
  4. Segues to "why we need a buffer op-amp" (T1.12).
Common confusion
  • R1 on top vs R2. Tip: the resistor output is measured across goes on top.
  • Loading effect forgotten. Load impedance must be > 10 × R2 for accuracy.
  • Using a divider for power (e.g., whole MCU). That's a regulator job, not a divider.
Verify understanding
  1. Vin=5V, R1=4.7k, R2=10k. Vout=? 3.40V
  2. What happens as R2→0? Vout→0 (short)
  3. Why won't this supply 50 mA stably? Load current drops output — need regulator
Reference: SparkFun — Voltage Dividers

Resistors — Everything Practical

Session 5 · 40 min
Goal: Read any 4-band colour code, pick wattage, explain series/parallel.
ColourBlackBrownRedOrangeYellowGreenBlueVioletGreyWhite
Digit0123456789
Example: Brown-Black-Orange-Gold = 1, 0, ×10³, ±5% = 10 kΩ. Yellow-Violet-Red-Gold = 4.7 kΩ.
Series: R = R1 + R2...
Parallel: 1/R = 1/R1 + 1/R2...  (or R1R2/(R1+R2) for two)
Power: P = I²R (always check)
Teach this way
  1. Dump a handful on the desk. Read 10. Time her. Drill until sub-10-seconds each.
  2. E12 values: 10, 12, 15, 18, 22, 27, 33, 39, 47, 56, 68, 82. Real world only uses these and decade multiples.
  3. Tolerance: ±5% gold, ±1% brown.
  4. Wattage: 1/8, 1/4 (default), 1/2, 1, 2 W.
  5. SMD uses numeric codes: "103" = 10 kΩ, "4R7" = 4.7 Ω.
Common confusion
  • 4-band vs 5-band. Check bands count first.
  • Reading backward. Tolerance band (gold/silver/brown) should be on right.
  • Missing wattage check.
Verify understanding
  1. Red-Red-Brown-Gold? 220 Ω ±5%
  2. Two 10k in parallel? 5 kΩ
  3. Need 75 Ω from E12? 82 Ω
T1.6

Capacitors — Types, Values, Polarity

Session 6 · 40 min
Goal: Pick the right cap type (decoupling, filtering, timing); explain why polarity matters.
TypeValuesPolar?Use
Ceramic (MLCC)1 pF – 10 μFNoDecoupling, filtering
Electrolytic1 μF – 10 mFYesBulk power filter
Tantalum0.1 μF – 1 mFYesLow-noise rails
Film1 nF – 10 μFNoAudio, precision timing
Q = C · V    XC = 1/(2πfC)
Why 100nF near every IC? At 100 MHz noise, XC of 100nF = 0.016 Ω. Effectively a short. Shunts noise to GND. Non-negotiable.
Teach this way
  1. Show 4 real caps side-by-side: ceramic disc, MLCC chip, aluminium electrolytic, tantalum.
  2. Marking: ceramic 3-digit code (104 = 100 nF); electrolytics labelled μF + voltage.
  3. Polarity: stripe on electrolytic = negative. Reverse = explosion.
  4. Voltage rating: ≥ 2× the expected. 16V cap on 5V, 25V on 12V.
  5. Decoupling pattern: 100nF + 10μF parallel on every power pin.
Common confusion
  • Thinking bigger is always better. Ceramic 100nF for HF; electrolytic 100μF for LF bulk. Use both.
  • Electrolytics backwards — literal explosions. Verify stripe.
  • Under-voltage cap — marginal reliability.
Verify understanding
  1. Cap marked "104" — value? 100 nF (0.1 μF)
  2. Which type closest to IC Vcc pin? Ceramic 100 nF — low impedance at HF
  3. Minimum voltage rating for 12V rail? 25V (2× margin)
T1.7

RC Circuits & Time Constant

Session 7 · 45 min
Goal: Sketch charging/discharging curves, compute τ=RC, explain debouncing with RC low-pass.

Charging a cap through a resistor: V rises exponentially. At 1τ, V = 63% of Vin. At 5τ, >99%. Shows up in: reset circuits, switch debouncing, audio filters, 555 timers.

τ = R · C [s, with R in Ω and C in F]
Charge: V(t) = Vin(1 − e−t/τ)   Discharge: V(t) = V0e−t/τ
Reset: R=10k, C=10nF, τ=100μs. 500μs charges >99%. Typical MCU reset.
Teach this way
  1. Breadboard: 9V, switch, 100k, 100μF, GND. τ=10s — visible timing.
  2. Toggle switch; measure Vcap every 2s with DMM. Plot the curve.
  3. Verify: at t=10s, V = 9(1−1/e) = 5.7V.
  4. Show switch-bounce scope trace. Add 10k + 100nF. Smooth. Explains anti-bounce.
Common confusion
  • Thinking charge is linear. It's exponential — draw both for contrast.
  • Unit arithmetic: kΩ×μF=ms, MΩ×μF=s.
  • Low-pass (output across cap) vs high-pass (across R). Draw both.
Verify understanding
  1. R=1k, C=10μF. τ=? 10 ms
  2. Time to reach >99%? 5τ = 50 ms
  3. Want 1s reset. Pick R, C. R=100k, C=10μF
T1.8

Diodes & LEDs

Session 8 · 35 min
Goal: Explain forward/reverse bias, Vf, breakdown; size current-limit for any LED.

One-way current valve. Forward bias (anode > cathode by Vf) conducts. Reverse biased, blocks until breakdown.

Vf to memorise: Si = 0.7V, Schottky = 0.3V, red LED = 2.0V, green = 2.2V, blue/white = 3.0–3.4V.

Diode I-V. Not ohmic — exponential above Vf.
LED current-limit: R = (Vsupply − Vf) / If
Teach this way
  1. Flip LED around in blink circuit. Nothing lights. Polarity matters.
  2. Check-valve analogy.
  3. Zener: reverse breakdown as voltage ref. Show 5.1V Zener circuit.
  4. Schottky: low Vf for reverse-polarity protection on 3.3V rails.
  5. Show a simple reverse-polarity protection schematic snippet.
Common confusion
  • Applying V=IR across LED.
  • Vf "constant" — varies slightly with I, but use typical from datasheet.
  • Cathode marking: LED shorter leg, diode stripe.
Verify understanding
  1. Why doesn't 5V backwards light an LED? Reverse biased — blocks
  2. Lower Vf: Schottky or Si? Schottky (~0.3V)
  3. 5.1V reference from 12V? Zener + current-limit R
T1.9

BJT as a Switch

Session 9 · 40 min
Goal: Drive a relay from a 3.3V GPIO with NPN BJT; size base resistor.

Three regions: cutoff (off), active (amplifier, IC=βIB), saturation (fully on, VCE~0.2V). For switching, push hard into saturation.

NPN most common. 2N2222 (TO-92, 800 mA). For higher current, use MOSFET.

RB = (VGPIO − VBE) / IB   where IB = IC/β
Rule of thumb: IB = IC/10 (over-drive for saturation)
Drive 100mA relay from 3.3V GPIO, β=100. IB=10mA. RB=(3.3−0.7)/0.01=260Ω → 220Ω.
Teach this way
  1. Build LED + BJT + base R. Drive from Arduino GPIO. MCU controls LED through transistor.
  2. For pure LED, GPIO can drive directly. But for motors, relays, heaters, current exceeds GPIO → need switch.
  3. Flyback diode: inductive loads (relay, motor) need reverse-biased diode across load. Spike kills transistor otherwise.
Common confusion
  • NPN/PNP arrow direction. "NPN Never Points iN."
  • Missing base R — damages GPIO.
  • Missing flyback diode on inductive loads.
  • Exceeding transistor rating (2N2222 is 800mA, not 2A).
Verify understanding
  1. 3.3V GPIO, 50mA load, 2N2222, β=100. RB? IB=5mA; RB=520Ω→470Ω
  2. Why over-drive base? Guarantee saturation across variation
  3. Across relay coil? Flyback diode, cathode to V+
T1.10

MOSFET as a Switch

Session 10 · 40 min
Goal: Pick BJT vs MOSFET; understand why "logic-level" matters for 3.3V MCUs.

Voltage-controlled (unlike BJT). Three specs matter: VGS(th), RDS(on), ID max.

N-channel low-side (source to GND), P-channel high-side. For 3.3V GPIO driving ground-referenced load, use logic-level N-MOSFET (e.g., IRLZ44N, RDS(on)~22mΩ at VGS=5V).

Gate R: 100–1k (damp ringing)
Pull-down: 10k gate-to-source (off when GPIO tri-state)
Pdiss,on = ID² · RDS(on)
Switch 2A motor from 3.3V GPIO. IRLZ44N: P = 4×0.022 = 88mW. No heatsink. BJT couldn't handle this.
Teach this way
  1. Compare 2N2222 (100mA) vs IRLZ44N (47A). Same package, orders-of-magnitude more capable.
  2. Draw low-side N-MOSFET switching. Gate R + pull-down.
  3. Emphasise "logic level": non-logic needs VGS=10V. 3.3V won't fully turn on → burns up.
  4. For high-side, use P-channel with NPN driver.
Common confusion
  • Missing RDS(on) vs VGS curve reading. Spec at VGS=10V can be 5× worse at 3.3V.
  • Forgetting gate pull-down.
  • No flyback on inductive loads.
  • Confusing S/G/D.
Verify understanding
  1. 5A LED strip from ESP32. BJT or MOSFET? Logic-level N-MOSFET
  2. "Logic level" means? Fully on at 3.3V or 5V, not 10V
  3. Why 10k gate-to-source? Keeps off when GPIO floats
T1.11

Op-Amps — The Golden Rules

Session 11 · 45 min
Goal: Apply two golden rules to derive any standard config in 30 seconds.

Rule 1: V+ = V− (because gain is huge, feedback pulls them equal).

Rule 2: Iin,+ = Iin,− = 0 (input impedance infinite for ideal).

With these + NFB, every config drops out in a line.

Op-amp symbol. Power rails usually omitted.
1. V+ = V−    2. Iin = 0
Teach this way
  1. Open-loop first: 100,000+ gain. Tiny V difference slams to rail. Only useful as comparator.
  2. Close the loop → op-amp self-adjusts to V+=V−. Magic.
  3. LM358: hobbyist op-amp. Cheap, 5V single-supply friendly.
  4. Cover practical limits (output swing, bandwidth) lightly — focus on two rules.
Common confusion
  • V+ vs +Vs (supply) confused. Different pins.
  • Rules need NFB to apply. Comparator doesn't have NFB.
  • Forgetting supplies connected.
Verify understanding
  1. V+=1.5V, NFB active. V−? 1.5V
  2. Current into V+ pin (ideal)? Zero
  3. Can standard op-amp output swing to 0V? Only if rail-to-rail. Standard can't exactly.
T1.12

Op-Amp Configs (Buffer, Inverting, Non-Inverting, Comparator)

Session 12 · 50 min
Goal: Recognise 4 canonical configs in any schematic and compute gain.
  • Buffer (follower): gain=1. Isolates high-Z source from low-Z load.
  • Non-inverting: gain = 1 + Rf/Rg. Min gain = 1.
  • Inverting: gain = −Rf/Rin. Can attenuate.
  • Comparator: no NFB. Output slams high or low. Threshold detection.
Buffer: Vout = Vin
Non-inv: Vout = Vin(1 + Rf/Rg)
Inv: Vout = −Vin(Rf/Rin)
Comparator: Vout = +Vs if V+>V−, else −Vs
Sensor scaling: 0–1V pressure sensor → 0–3.3V ADC. Non-inv gain 3.3. Rf=23k, Rg=10k. Use standard Rf=22k, Rg=10k → gain 3.2. Calibrate in software.
Teach this way
  1. LM358 non-inverting gain=2 on breadboard. Pot input on 5V. Watch output double.
  2. Sweep 0–2V input; output follows. Tangible.
  3. Swap to inverting. With single-supply, pivot around mid-rail reference.
  4. Buffer: Vout→V−. Gain=1 but drives loads that crushed divider output.
  5. Comparator: no feedback. Threshold-detector LED.
Common confusion
  • Rf/Rg swapped. Rg to GND, Rf to Vout.
  • Non-inv min gain = 1 (not less).
  • Single-supply needs mid-rail reference for non-inv input.
Verify understanding
  1. Non-inv Rf=47k, Rg=4.7k. Gain? 11
  2. Want 1/3 attenuation. Config? Inverting
  3. Config with no feedback? Comparator
T1.13

Digital Logic — Gates, Flip-Flops, Counters

Session 13 · 50 min
Goal: Reproduce truth tables for AND/OR/NOT/NAND/XOR; explain flip-flop and ripple counter.

Boolean operations on binary signals. She has the theory; connect to ICs: 7400 NAND, 7404 inverter, 7474 D-FF, 74HC595 shift register.

Modern: most gates are inside MCU/CPLD/FPGA. But recognition of 74xx in older designs matters.

See Logic Gates section on Symbols Reference for visuals.

GateTruth (00/01/10/11)
AND0 / 0 / 0 / 1
OR0 / 1 / 1 / 1
NOTinverts
NAND1 / 1 / 1 / 0
NOR1 / 0 / 0 / 0
XOR0 / 1 / 1 / 0

D flip-flop: on each clock edge, latches D to Q. Basis of registers / counters.

Ripple counter: N flip-flops chained, each divides by 2. 8 bits → 0–255.

Teach this way
  1. Fill truth tables blindly after explanation.
  2. "NAND is universal": build NOT, AND, OR from only NANDs.
  3. Flip-flop: timing diagram, clock edges, D/Q.
  4. 74HC595 shift register: real-world FF example, drives many LEDs from few pins.
Common confusion
  • Level vs edge triggered. Modern = edge.
  • Active-low signals (/RESET).
  • Floating CMOS inputs pick up noise. Tie unused high/low.
Verify understanding
  1. XOR truth? 00,01,10,11 → 0,1,1,0
  2. AND from NANDs? NAND + NOT (another NAND with inputs tied)
  3. 8-bit ripple counter divides 16 MHz to? 16M/256 = 62.5 kHz
