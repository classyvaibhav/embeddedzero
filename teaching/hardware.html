T2.1

Schematic Symbols — Using the Reference

Session 14 · 30 min
Goal: She recognises every symbol in a typical ESP32 reference schematic at a glance.

Before reading real schematics, she needs vocabulary. We've already built the Symbols Reference page — 32 components with inline SVG graphics. Use it for:

  • Recognition drill: Open the page, cover the names, show only SVGs. She names them in <2 seconds.
  • Parallel tab during schematic reading: Whenever an unfamiliar symbol appears, open Symbols page in a side-tab.
  • Quiz review weekly: 10 random symbols, she names them. Over 6 weeks this becomes muscle memory.
Teach this way
  1. Open symbols.html with her. Scroll through each of the 6 sections (Passives, Diodes, Transistors, ICs, Power/Ground, Logic Gates).
  2. Then open a real schematic — Arduino Uno schematic is ideal.
  3. Point to 5 random symbols. She names them. Do this for 15 minutes.
  4. Emphasise net labels (VCC, GND, RX) — signals with same name are connected even without visible wire.
Common confusion
  • NPN vs PNP arrow direction — drill until automatic.
  • Polar vs non-polar cap — one curved line is the tell.
  • Signal GND vs Chassis GND vs Earth GND — physically different, look different.
  • Missing net labels: same name = wired together.
Reference: Internal Symbols Page · SparkFun — How to Read a Schematic

Reading a Full Schematic — Block by Block

Session 15 · 60 min
Goal: Given any embedded board schematic, she identifies power block, MCU, reset, crystal, I/O headers, and communication interfaces.

Every embedded schematic has the same building blocks. Teach her to find them in order:

Every embedded schematic maps to this pattern.
Teach this way
  1. Download ESP32 DevKit v1 schematic (Espressif publishes it).
  2. Give her 5 minutes to try identifying blocks — silently.
  3. Then walk through block-by-block. Show USB → CP2102 → UART → ESP32 signal chain.
  4. Ask: "What happens when EN is pressed?" → trace the reset.
  5. Next session, repeat with Arduino Uno schematic. Same structure, different parts.
  6. Eventually, show her an Aimtron-style dashcam schematic. Connects her internship to real design.
Common confusion
  • Missing where a signal starts / ends. Follow net labels, not just wires.
  • Not noticing decoupling caps. Emphasise: no decoupling → broken board.
  • Confusing "3V3" / "V3" / other shorthand.
Verify understanding
  1. What regulates USB 5V to 3.3V on ESP32 DevKit? AMS1117-3.3 LDO
  2. How does PC talk to ESP32? CP2102 USB-to-UART bridge → ESP32 UART0
  3. What does pressing EN do? Pulls EN low → ESP32 resets
T2.3

Power Supply Blocks — LDO vs Buck

Session 16 · 40 min
Goal: Pick between LDO and switching regulator; read "typical application circuit" from datasheet.

LDO (linear): Simple, quiet, cheap, low parts count. But wastes P = (Vin−Vout) × I as heat. Only good for small drops. AMS1117-3.3, LM7805.

Switching regulator (buck): ~90% efficient, handles big drops. More parts (inductor, switching noise). MP1584, LM2596, TPS54xx.

Rule of thumb: if Vdrop × I > 1 W, use buck.

LDO Pdiss: (Vin − Vout) · Iload
Buck efficiency: 85–95% typical
7.4V Li-Ion → 3.3V at 200mA. LDO: 0.82W hot. Buck: 0.07W loss. Buck wins for battery life.
Teach this way
  1. Open AMS1117-3.3 datasheet. Find "typical application" page. 10μF in + 10μF out. Done.
  2. Then MP1584: inductor, feedback divider, Schottky, caps. Explain each.
  3. Dropout: AMS1117 needs Vin ≥ Vout + 1.2V. 4V min for 3.3V.
  4. Thermal: >100°C → add heatsink or switch topology.
Common confusion
  • Using LDO for big drops — cooks. Calculate P first.
  • Missing output cap — LDO oscillates.
  • Buck inductor far from IC — EMI. Keep loop tight.
Verify understanding
  1. 12V in, 5V out, 1A. LDO or buck? Buck (LDO dissipates 7W)
  2. What does "low dropout" mean? Vin–Vout can be small before regulation fails
  3. Why is inductor placement critical? Small loop = lower EMI
T2.4

How to Read a Datasheet

Session 17 · 50 min
Goal: Given an unfamiliar datasheet, find pinout, abs max, operating conditions, and application circuit in <2 min.

Datasheets look intimidating (100+ pages) but 90% of what she needs is pages 1–5. Systematic approach:

  1. Front page: part number, description, features, packages. Confirms right part.
  2. Pinout: usually page 2. Memorise Vcc, GND, data lines.
  3. Absolute max ratings: destruction limits. Never exceed.
  4. Recommended operating conditions: normal ranges.
  5. Electrical characteristics: VOH/VOL, IOH/IOL, current, frequency.
  6. Typical application circuit: free reference schematic. Copy it exactly.
  7. Timing diagrams: for I2C/SPI chips, shows setup/hold.
Teach this way
  1. Pick an unfamiliar sensor (BMP280). Don't prep. Open datasheet.
  2. 5 min: she finds pinout, Vcc range, I2C address, typical circuit. Watch silently.
  3. Walk through together, discuss what she got right/wrong.
  4. Next session: repeat with STM32F103, AMS1117, LM358. Each has slightly different structure.
  5. "Typical application circuit" is free engineering. Start from datasheet ref, modify.
Common confusion
  • Absolute max read as nominal. Abs max = destruction, not normal.
  • Package-specific pinouts. DIP vs SOIC different.
  • Conditional specs. "At Vcc=5V, T=25°C" — changes elsewhere.
Verify understanding
  1. BMP280 Vcc range? I2C address options? 1.71–3.6V; 0x76 or 0x77
  2. Is absolute max the safe ceiling? No — destruction threshold
  3. Always on page 1? Part number, features, packages
Reference: Phil's Lab — How to Read a Datasheet
T2.5

Component Selection Process

Session 18 · 45 min
Goal: Given "I need X", she finds 3 candidates on DigiKey, compares, picks one with justification.

Real selection isn't finding a part — it's tradeoffs: price vs availability vs performance vs footprint vs lifecycle.

  1. Translate to specs: "LDO" → Vin, Vout, Iout, dropout, package.
  2. Filter search on DigiKey. Narrow to <20 candidates.
  3. Sort by stock. 16-week lead-time part is useless.
  4. Lifecycle: Active / NRND / Obsolete.
  5. Compare top 3 side-by-side.
  6. Always alternate MPN — drop-in if primary out.
Teach this way
  1. Assign: "Find 3 candidates for 3.3V 500mA LDO, SOT-223, <₹20, in stock."
  2. Watch her search. Note filter choices.
  3. Walk comparison: dropout, quiescent, thermal, price, alternates.
  4. Show Octopart for cross-vendor compare.
  5. Show Robu.in for India-local sourcing.
  6. Practice weekly — 15 min exercise per week.
Common confusion
  • Cheapest without checking stock/lifecycle.
  • Wrong package (SOT-223 vs SOT-89).
  • Ignoring temp range (commercial vs industrial vs automotive).
Verify understanding
  1. NRND on Mouser — use for new design? No
  2. Alternate for AMS1117-3.3? LM1117-3.3, LD1117-3.3
  3. Why industrial grade for Gujarat factory? 45°C+ ambient; commercial marginal above 70°C
Tools: DigiKey India · Octopart · Robu.in

Teach this way
  1. Give her ESP32 DevKit schematic + blank template. ~30 rows.
  2. First pass: 90 min. Review.
  3. Catch: missing alternates, wrong footprints, missing notes.
  4. Show a real Aimtron BOM if you can access.

  • Forgetting footprint.

  2. Is one-row grouping OK? Yes, list all designators
  3. Why mark DNP? Prevents assembly placing unused parts

T2.8

Reset & Boot Circuits

Session 21 · 30 min
Goal: Draw an MCU reset circuit; explain ESP32 EN vs BOOT; debug a board that won't reset.

Every MCU needs a clean reset pulse on power-up. Simplest: pull-up (~10k) on /RESET + button to GND. Sometimes 100nF cap for POR delay.

ESP32: EN = enable/reset; BOOT = held low while EN pulsed to enter bootloader. CP2102 on dev kit automates this.

Minimal reset circuit: pull-up + button to GND.
Teach this way
  1. Draw simple reset on whiteboard.
  2. Explain EN vs BOOT on ESP32 schematic. See the two transistors from DTR/RTS.
  3. Common failure: no pull-up on /RESET → floats → random resets.
  4. Mention brown-out detection (BOD) on ESP32.
Common confusion
  • No pull-up. /RESET must have defined state.
  • Too-large cap — delays reset beyond first clock cycles.
  • Active-low vs high. Most are active-low (bar over /RESET).
Verify understanding
  1. Bar over /RESET? Active-low
  2. Why ESP32 needs both EN and BOOT? EN=reset; BOOT state at reset selects bootloader
  3. Board won't reset — 3 things to check? Pull-up, button, Vcc
