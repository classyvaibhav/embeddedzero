01Student Context

Everything below is tuned for Bahar's specific starting point. Adjust if you teach someone else later.

Who Bahar Is (for teaching purposes)

  • MSc Applied Physics — strong in physics fundamentals (EM theory, semiconductor band structure, optics, numerical methods).
  • Electronics was an elective, so her gates/flip-flops/op-amps knowledge is theoretical but not intuitive.
  • Python programmer for numerical work, but C/C++ is new.
  • 1-week Aimtron internship — has touched a real PCB, tested dashcams, seen Laplacian/Sobel calibration. Leverage this context constantly.
  • Target is an Embedded Systems Trainee role, not a firmware dev role. Her bar is "understand what an Arduino sketch does and can modify it", not "write interrupt handlers from scratch".

02Teaching Principles

  • Build first, explain second. Wire the circuit on a breadboard / in Tinkercad before deriving the formula. She needs physical intuition before symbolic reasoning — otherwise it sits in her head as another exam topic.
  • One concept per session. Don't stack topics. A focused 45-minute session on "voltage divider" beats a 2-hour kitchen-sink session.
  • End every session with three check-questions. If she can't answer all three, plan a 15-minute review at the start of next session. No moving on silently.
  • Use her physics background as leverage. When explaining PN junctions, op-amps, or signal conditioning, anchor in physics she already knows (semiconductor band theory, circuit analysis).
  • Avoid bare-metal firmware rabbit holes. You know the deep NXP/Yocto stack — resist the urge to show it. She needs Arduino-level confidence first.
  • Gotcha callouts first. In every topic card, there's a gotcha section. Read that before the session so you know what to emphasise.
  • Link theory to Aimtron. When you cover camera calibration or PCB inspection, explicitly point at her internship. Keeps the relevance high.

0345-Minute Session Template

Minute 0–5: Recap

Ask last session's check-questions again. Catches concepts that didn't stick after 24–48h of forgetting.

Minute 5–15: Concept

Whiteboard / paper. Use the diagram in the topic card. Don't skip the worked example with real numbers.

Minute 15–35: Hands-on

Breadboard or Tinkercad. She drives; you guide. If hardware isn't available, Tinkercad Circuits or Wokwi are free and fine.

Minute 35–45: Check + Homework

Ask the 3 check-questions. Assign a small task for her to attempt alone before the next session.

04Hardware You'll Need

  • ESP32 DevKit v1 — ~₹450 — primary MCU for this curriculum
  • Arduino Uno R3 — ~₹500 — easier for first circuits (5V tolerant, mature ecosystem)
  • Breadboard 830-point + jumper wire kit — ~₹300
  • Component assortment — ~₹600–800 total:
    • Resistor pack (10 Ω to 1 MΩ, 1/4 W)
    • Capacitor pack (ceramic + electrolytic)
    • LEDs (3 mm / 5 mm, various colours)
    • Push buttons, 10 kΩ potentiometer, slide switches
    • Transistors: 2N2222 (NPN), 2N3906 (PNP), IRLZ44N (logic-level N-MOSFET)
    • Op-amp: LM358
    • Sensors: DHT11, BMP280, 0.96" OLED (I2C)
    • Misc: 1N4148 diode, 1N5819 Schottky, voltage regulator AMS1117-3.3
  • Digital multimeter — she has one in the Applied Physics lab
  • Oscilloscope — lab access is enough
  • USB cables, jumper wires (M-M, M-F, F-F)

Total one-time spend ~₹1800. Buy from Robu.in (cheapest, reliable for Gujarat) or Amazon.in. Typical arrival: 3–5 days.

05Software Setup

  • Arduino IDE 2.x — install on her laptop. Add ESP32 board package via Preferences → Additional Board URLs.
  • KiCad 8.x — for reading/modifying PCB projects later (Week 8).
  • VS Code + PlatformIO extension — optional upgrade from Arduino IDE once she's comfortable.
  • Git + GitHub — non-negotiable. Create her account session 1.
  • Browser tabs she should always have open:
    • This teaching guide (Symbols page in particular)
    • DigiKey India for parts lookup
    • Wokwi for browser simulation
    • Random Nerd Tutorials for ESP32 examples
